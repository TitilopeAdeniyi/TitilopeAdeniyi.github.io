## Goals
Exams are finished. Research begins. Dr. Sun has been an amazing guide and resource even before starting this project. During the school year I was able to get a copy of the proposal and began light reading and practicing my researching skills before May. It was much needed.
Going in, I needed to figure out how I wanted to organize my files, writing, papers, and handwritten notes, and get myself used to long hours in the lab versus the long hours I spend studying for classes.
The research goal for the week was to close a big information gap: what do trials and patient information actually look like? Two questions drove it — how many trials will I need to pull for my experiment, and how will I find the patients needed to test the AI model I will use to generate the pairs?
## Approach/Implementation
Downloading and understanding the Citrix Workspace was easy, and my Operating Systems course paid off since the Knuth server works the same way. The department SysAdmin also helped me.
I tried my hand at downloading files from the ClinicalTrials.gov API. They are hard to read and use technical language beyond my scope. The medical language I do not have to worry about too much — my focus is on the general language used to look for willing volunteers for the trials, and I can understand that from the .json files.
For the patient side, I weighed two options: look for relevant papers published in the last five years and use their paired data to train my model, or attempt to pair the data myself.
# Results
The first option is the best one. All I need are real patient cases that have been paired with multiple trials. I can make some changes to fit my project and create my own test cases with patients and trials that still exist on the site.
Real data is hard to get and verify. There are many datasets out there that could have been synthetically generated. That is fine for practice, but real patient data is raw and dirty, sometimes incoherent and always necessary that every all details are taken down. The system changing isn't the goal of my research, so my job is to adapt to it. I used to work at a Medical clinic so I have first hand on how patients give information and how medical professional document them. I was never trained to pick up imposssible data, but again mistakes in data are the best tests for my project. It should be able to handle it and give insights. I'm not worried too much on patient side. The Trial side has the most walls and authorization to deal with.
